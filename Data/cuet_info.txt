1.1 POSITIVE FEEDBACK AND THE BARKHAUSEN CRITERION
- Positive feedback is regenerative and reinforces change, making it essential for the construction of oscillators.
- The Barkhausen Oscillation Criterion states that to maintain self-oscillation:
  1. The closed-loop gain (product of open-loop gain A and feedback factor β) must be unity or greater: |Aβ| ≥ 1.
  2. The loop phase shift must be an integer multiple of 360° (or 0°): Loop Phase = N * 360°, where N = 0, 1, 2, 3...
- If the loop gain falls below unity or the feedback signal is not perfectly in phase, oscillation will cease.

1.2 PRACTICAL CONSIDERATIONS FOR OSCILLATORS
- Start-up Signal: No external start-up signal source is needed. Broad-spectrum energy present in either circuit input noise or a turn-on transient contains the target frequency and kicks off the oscillation.
- Gain Control: To minimize distortion and prevent hard clipping/clipping limits, a practical oscillator needs an automatic gain limit/detecting network that rolls the closed-loop gain back to exactly 1 as the signal amplitude rises.
- Frequency Selectivity & Accuracy: To minimize frequency drift over time, the feedback network should be selective (having a high quality factor, Q), such as RLC tank circuits or piezoelectric crystals, attenuating non-target frequencies.

1.3 WIEN BRIDGE OSCILLATOR PATHS
- Employs a frequency-sensitive lead/lag voltage divider network.
- Feedback factor equation: 
  β = Z2 / (Z1 + Z2)
  where Z1 = R1 - jXc1  and  Z2 = R2 || -jXc2

  * Attenuation at Resonance: β = 1 / 3
  * Forward Amplifier Requirements: Must have a phase shift of 0° (non-inverting) and a gain slightly greater than 3 to initiate and exactly 3 to maintain low-distortion oscillation.

EXERCISE 3.1: Proving Circuit Failures via the Barkhausen Conditions
- Scenario A: In a Wien bridge oscillator, forward gain drops below 3.
  * Result: The Aβ loop product falls below unity (|Aβ| < 1). Oscillation cannot sustain itself and decays to zero.
- Scenario B: Replace one of the inverting amplifiers in a dual-stage basic oscillator (Figure 9.3) with a non-inverting amplifier of equal gain.
  * Result: The original loop phase was -180° + -180° = -360° (0°). Introducing a non-inverting stage leaves the total loop phase at 180°. This breaks the perfect in-phase condition required for regenerative feedback, immediately halting oscillation.

EXERCISE 3.2: Op-Amp Slew Rate and Bandwidth Performance Effects
- High-frequency limitations of standard op-amps (e.g., UA741 vs LF411/LF411CN) introduces parasitic phase shifts.
- Parasitic phase shift violates the assumption of a pure non-inverting/inverting stage block, shifting the true oscillation point away from the mathematical target.
- Slew rate limiting in square/triangle wave generators introduces non-negligible transitions delays:
  * Distorts triangle waves by rounding off peak thresholds.
  * Causes square wave transitions to skew into trapezoids.
  * Delays state switching times, leading to measured output frequencies that drop significantly lower than calculated equations predict (e.g., a 741 drop of 15% below target in a 2 kHz design context).
